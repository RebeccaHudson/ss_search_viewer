  1. Understanding motif libraries

    2. Motif libraries provide transcription factor binding nucleotide profiles from a set of transcription factor binding sites. The binding profiles are represented with position weight matrices (PWMs). PWMs are used for probabilistic description of the patterns in genomic sequences.

    • JASPAR 2014 library

      • JASPAR (http://jaspar2014.genereg.net/) is the largest open-access database of matrix-based nucleotide profiles describing the binding preference of transcription factors from multiple species (Mathelier et al., 2013). atSNP uses JASPAR 2014 CORE Vertebrata to evaluate impact of SNPs on transcription factor binding. They provide position frequency matrices for 205 non-redundant profiles and their summaries. The position frequency matrix is easily converted to the position weight matrix by dividing nucleotide frequencies by their total frequency. The PWM is visualized as a sequence logo, where logo heights are scaled based on their information content. JASPR library provides a summary page for each motif.

      Example
      JASPAR library contains a position frequency matrix for a transcription factor RUNX1 and its sequence logo as below. Its summary is presented here (link: http://jaspar2014.genereg.net/cgi-bin/jaspar_db.pl?ID=MA0002.2&rm=present&collection=CORE.)

    • ENCODE Library
      • ENCODE library features systematic discovery and characterization of regulatory motifs in ENCODE transcription factor binding experiments available at http://compbio.mit.edu/encode-motifs/. They collected known motifs from the literature (TRANSFAC, JASPAR Homo Sapiens, and UniPROBE). Further, they discovered motifs de novo on hundreds of transcription factor binding experiments in human ENCODE using established tools such as MDscan , MEME, Trawler, and AlignACE (Kheradpour et al., 2014). All known and discovered motifs are grouped as in the table at http://compbio.mit.edu/encode-motifs/. The names of ENCODE motifs contain their information on which literature reports the known motifs or which tool was used to obtain de novo motifs.
      Example

      ENCODE library provides 22 known motifs and 6 discovered motifs for transcription factor GATA, which are available at http://compbio.mit.edu/encode-motifs/tf.html?GATA. Here, we present one known GATA motif (GATA_known12) and one discovered GATA motif (GATA_disc1). GATA_known12 is from JASPAR Homo Sapiens and GATA_disc1 is obtained from ENCODE Snyder Lab GATA TF experiments with MDscan tool.

  2. at-SNP in-silico statistical method
    3. atSNP overview

    atSNP takes input as SNP information/genomic subsequences and a PWM for a transcription factor. atSNP uses first order Markov model and importance sampling technique to test the goodness of match between the PWM and the subsequences overlapping the SNP position on both reference and SNP alleles and the impact of SNP on the change in the goodness of match. The testing results are summarized with a table and a graphical output called a composite logo plot. To learn more about the outputs, visit "Interpreting search output” and “Reading composite logo plot”.

    Table 1 provides functionalities of atSNP statistical method and alternative methods. atSNP provides the most comprehensive information on identifying impact of SNPs on transcription factor binding. Only atSNP and is-rSNP provide statistical quantification on the impact of SNPs on the binding. Further, atSNP addresses the scalability issue with its efficient computation and visualizes the best matches on both reference and SNP alleles and their change created by the SNP.

    atSNP Procedure
    • Step 1: affinity score

      • Use the PWM of each motif as the product multinomial parameters and compute log likelihoods for all subsequences of the motif length overlapping the SNP location. Find the best matches with the highest log likelihood on both SNP and reference alleles. The scores are used for statistical testing.

      Example
      We evaluate impact of a SNP, rs117959046, on transcription factor GATA2 binding, with GATA_known12 motif:

      The motif length is 5 and sequences of length 9 centered at the SNP position are extracted from both reference and SNP genomes:

      Reference allele: AAGACAAGC
      SNP allele: AAGATAAGC

      Next, we compute log likelihoods of all subsequences of length 5 spanning the SNP position and find the best match, which gives the largest log likelihood. The second subsequences on the forward strand have the largest log likelihood on both reference and SNP alleles (Tables 2-3).

    • Step 2: p-value for the affinity score

      • The p-value for the affinity score on the reference allele (pref) is the probability that a random sequence has the affinity score larger than the best match score on the reference allele. atSNP simulates random sequences from a first order Markov model and estimates the p-value as the frequency of getting larger affinity scores. Similarly, the p-value for the affinity score on the SNP allele (psnp) is estimated as the frequency of getting random scores larger than the best match score on the SNP allele.

      Example (Continued)
      From Step 1, the log likelihood score for the reference allele is -25.30, and the log likelihood score for the SNP allele is -2.28. The p-value for the reference allele is the probability that a random sequence has the affinity scores larger than or equal to -25.30, which is our target region (Figure 7). For such a small p-value, naive Monte-Carlo generates small number of scores in the target region (Figure 8), thus requires a large number of simulations. Alternatively, in atSNP, the importance sampling generates many more scores in the target region (Figure 9). The p-value for the reference allele is then estimated by the weighted frequency as pref=0.499. Importance sampling significantly reduces the required number of simulations. Similar to the reference allele, pSNP is estimated as 0.009.

    • Step 3: p-value for the affinity change between alleles

      • atSNP tests the significance of log rank ratio: log(pref)-log(psnp). atSNP computes the p-value based on Monte-Carlo simulation for both alleles with the importance sampling technique as in Step 2.

      Example (Continued)
      The log rank ratio for the impact of SNP, rs117959046, is 4.02. With the importance sampling, the p-value for the log rank ratio is estimated as prank=0.005.

      Reference
      • Zuo, Chandler, Shin, Sunyoung, and Keleş, Sündüz. atSNP: transcription factor binding affinity testing for regulatory SNP detection. Bioinformatics (2015): 31, 3353-3355

  3. Interpreting Search Output

    4. atSNP search outputs are grouped into four general categories: SNP information, motif information, atSNP scores, and atSNP p-values. Downloaded csv file provides additional information on the impact of SNP and the matches on both alleles. Specific details are summarized in the following tables.

    Chromosome Human genome 38 chromosome, e.g. Chr1-Chr22, ChrX, ChrY, ChrM
    Position 1-based location of the SNP relative to the first base at the 5’ end of the lead strand of the reference sequence 2
    Reference Allele Reference nucleotide
    SNP Allele SNP nucleotide

        3. Motif information
        Transcription Factor Transcription factor name, e.g. RUNX1
        Motif Motif name, e.g. MA0002.2

        atSNP Scores
        Log Likelihood Reference The log likelihood score of the best match on the reference allele
        Log Likelihood SNP The log likelihood score of the best match on the SNP allele
        Log Likelihood Ratio The difference between Log Likelihood Reference and Log Likelihood SNP

        atSNP p-values
        p-value Rank (prank) A significant p-value, e.g. 0.0001, statistically supports the impact of the SNP on the transcription binding affinity change. This is the p-value which most atSNP users are interested in.
        p-value Reference (pref) p-values for scores on the reference allele (Log Likelihood Reference). A significant p-value indicates that the match between the reference allele and the motif is statistically supported.
        p-value SNP (pSNP) p-value for scores on the SNP allele (Log Likelihood SNP). A significant p-value indicates that the match between the SNP allele and the motif is statistically supported.
        p-value Difference (pDiff) p-values for the difference in scores between the reference and the SNP alleles (Log Likelihood Ratio). This is the result of the likelihood ratio testing. Our validating experiments do not recommend the p-value for the evaluation of impact of SNPs.

        Additional Information (available from Downloaded file)
        Log Enhance Odds Log likelihood ratio between SNP allele and reference allele based on the best matching subsequence on the reference allele
        Log Reduce Odds Log-likelihood ratio between reference allele and SNP allele based on the best matching subsequence on the SNP allele
        p-value Condition Ref (pcond_ref) Conditional p-value for scores on the reference allele based on Log Enhance Odds
        p-value Condition SNP (pcond_SNP) Conditional p-value for scores on the SNP allele based on Log Reduce Odds
        Ref Strand Strand where the best match on the reference allele occurs (positive or negative)
        SNP Strand Strand where the best match on the SNP allele occurs (positive or negative)
        Ref Start Relative position of the start of the best matching subsequence on the reference allele (SNP position takes the 31st location.)
        SNP Start Relative position of the start of the best matching subsequence on the SNP allele (SNP position takes the 31st location.)
        Ref End Relative position of the end of the best matching subsequence on the reference allele (SNP position takes the 31st location.)
        SNP End Relative position of the end of the best matching subsequence on the SNP allele (SNP position takes the 31st location.)

  4. Reading a Composite Logo Plot

    5. atSNP DB provides a composite logo plot to visualize significant impacts of SNPs on transcription factor binding. Users can easily see whether SNPs enhance/disrupt the transcription factor binding and how significant the changes are. Examples below will help users to interpret the logo plot and understand the perturbation in the regulatory element.

    Examples

    The SNP, rs117959046, is highly likely to create a binding of transcription factor GATA2 on the genome. The composite logo plot illustrates this with the comparison between the best matches of GATA2_2 PWM on the reference and SNP alleles. Among subsequences of length 5 overlapping the SNP position on the reference allele, “AGACA” on the forward strand has the highest log likelihood score. The subsequence is referred to as “best match with the reference genome” and depicted on the second row with the corresponding PWM on the first row. Similarly, among subsequences of length 5 overlapping the SNP position on the SNP allele, “AGATA” on the forward strand is referred to as “best match with the SNP genome” and depicted on the third row with the corresponding PWM on the fourth row.

    The SNP, rs199894601, is highly likely to create a binding of transcription factor RUNX1 on the genome. The best match position on the SNP allele is different from that on the reference allele. Specifically, the best matches occur on different strand and genomic position (bp). The composite logo plot illustrates this with the comparison between the best matches of RUNX1 JASPAR PWM on the reference and SNP alleles. Among subsequences of length 11 overlapping the SNP position on the SNP allele, a subsequence starting from 3 bases before the SNP on the reverse strand has the highest log likelihood score. The subsequence is referred to as “best match with the SNP genome” and depicted on the third row with the corresponding PWM on the fourth row. Similarly, we can find “best match with the reference genome” and depict it on the second row with the corresponding PWM on the first row.

  5. atSNP DB Infrastructure

    6. atSNP DB is scale out systems architecture for three hundred billion records. As demand/storage/performance increase, we add additional boxes. Elastic search, a NoSQL database, utilizes a distributed scale out system design for scalable workloads at scale while distributing searches and indexes. API proxies requests from web page to database.
